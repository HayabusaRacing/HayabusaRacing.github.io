Summary of the Simulation

We used a combination of Newton's Second Law of motion and the quadratic drag force to model the velocity of the car as a function of time. The model only accounts for two forces: Constant thrust from the CO2 cartridge and quadratic air drag. \[F_{drag} = \frac{1}{2} \rho C_d A v^2 = k v^2\] \[F_{total} = m a = F_{thrust} - F_{drag}\] \[a = \frac{F_{total}}{m}\] \[\frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{F_{thrust} - kv^2}{m}\] This separable differential equation could be solved analytically, and turns out to form a tangent hyperbolic equation. On this page, we used the 4th-order Runge-Kutta method to numerically solve the differential equation and plot the velocity versus time graph.

We assumed that the affecting factors of the car's lap times are listed below:

  • Mass of the car
  • Aerodynamic drag
  • Aerodynamic lift
  • Thrust from the CO2 cartridge
  • Bearing friction
  • Wheel inertia
  • Cartridge-caused tipping moment
The following forces will be modeled as a function of time: \[F_{thrust}(t) \text{: the force created by the cartridge thrust}\] \[F_{drag}(t) \text{: the force created by aerodynamic drag}\] \[F_{lift}(t) \text{: the force created by aerodynamic lift}\] \[F_{friction}(t) \text{: the force created by bearing friction}\] In addition, the mass of the car is expected to alter with respect to time, as the cartridge is used up; thus, we will model the mass as a function of time: \[m(t) \text{: the mass of the car at time } t\] A single wheel's inertia could be considered as a virtual mass and will be a constant value. \[I_{fw} \text{: the front wheel's inertia}\] \[I_{rw} \text{: the rear wheel's inertia}\] The tipping moment caused by the cartridge directly reduces the efficiency of the thrust, and will be a constant value. \[\eta_{tip} \text{: the tipping moment efficiency factor}\] Now, when we combine all these forces, we can write the equation of the force applied to the car. \[F_{total}(t) = F_{thrust}(t) \cdot \eta_{tip} - F_{drag}(t) - F_{friction}(t)\] According to Newton's Second Law, the derivative of the velocity is proportional to the total force applied to the car, divided by the mass of the car. \[F=ma\] \[a=\frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{F_{total}(t)}{m(t)}\ = \frac{F_{thrust}(t) \cdot \eta_{tip} - F_{drag}(t) - F_{friction}(t)}{m(t)}\]
